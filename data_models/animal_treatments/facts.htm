TERMS OF REFERENCE (ToR) 
THE BROOKE EGYPT STRATEGIC REVIEW

I. Context and rationale
1.1 Context
The Brooke is a leading UK animal welfare charity dedicated to improving the lives of horses, donkeys and mules and the 
livelihoods of their owners working in the poorest parts of the world. 

There are an estimated 100 million working equine animals in the world and they are often the only source of income for 
many poor families, who depend on their working animal to survive. 
The Brooke – along with our partner organizations worldwide - supports mobile veterinary teams and community animal health workers who 
provide treatment to working animals and advice to their owners. 

Our community engagement specialists build the capacity of animal owners, local healers, farriers, saddlers, feed sellers, harness- and 
cart-makers to look after their animals’ needs in a sustainable way. 

We currently operate across 10 countries in Asia, Africa, Central America and the Middle East. 
We have over 800 staff working directly in the field. 

The Brooke’s mission is to do whatever will most improve the welfare of working horses, donkeys and mules through the alleviation of 
existing suffering and the development of equine welfare practices and facilities to prevent and reduce suffering in the future. 

In 2009 we reached 730,000 working horses and donkeys - benefiting over 3.7 million poor people who depend on them. 
The Brooke’s five years goal is to measurably improve the welfare of at least two million of the working horses, donkeys and mules in 
the most need in the developing world.

Brooke Egypt (BE)
There are 1,202,000 equine animals working in Egypt as indicated by the official governmental census report of 2008. 
Most of the working equines across Egypt carry out a wide range of types of work under very difficult circumstances and are exposed to 
high risk of poor animal welfare. 

The majority is used for transportation of passengers such as tourist carts in Luxor, carrying loads such as domestic waste materials for
vulnerable communities like the Zabballin, and many thousands work in brick kilns, for example in Helwan near Cairo. 

The main welfare problems are overloading, beating, mutilation (mainly firing), dehydration, heat stress, lameness and hoof problems and contagious 
diseases such as equine influenza. Veterinary services are available through the local governmental veterinary clinics and private vet clinics. 
However many animal owners perceive accessibility to and affordability of or possibly need for veterinary services as a problem. 
The Brooke began in Egypt in 1934 and our work has expanded to help working animals in poor urban and rural communities across the country. 

The goal of Brooke Egypt is to reduce pain and suffering of working equines in Egypt, with an underlying objective to improve the welfare of 
working equines sustainably within Brooke Egypt’s operational area. 


Today, the Brooke has 30 community mobile veterinary clinical teams and a growing community animal health training programme. 
Apart from the original clinic in Cairo there are five other clinics. Alexandria was opened in 1961, Luxor in 1966, 
Aswan in 1988, Edfu in 1992, Mersah Matrouh in 2000 and in 2006 Brooke Egypt opened a base in the Nile Delta. 

The Brooke Egypt provided 313900  treatments last year and estimates it will reach a maximum of 100,500  working animals and animals’ users and carers this year.

1.2 Rationale
The Brooke international strategy, set within the 5 year organizational strategy, provides the framework for country programme development. 

Country programme strategies and annual action plans are developed within this framework, contributing to the strategic objectives of Brooke. 

In Brooke Egypt the attention of the programme and investment has been focused on providing free veterinary treatment through the static and mobile clinics. 

More recently, over the past 5 years, ad-hoc additional activities have been undertaken such as community engagement, working in partnership with CBOs and 
other local structures such as rural guides. Staff capacity has been built in the use of welfare assessment and research methodologies to measure whether 
animal welfare is improving. 

However external clinical audits and other informal reviews and processes have identified issues that need to be addressed systematically for reasons of 
animal welfare, for sound programming reasons and also because evidence suggests that the Brooke Egypt programme is out of alignment with the Brooke’s 
global strategic plan and framework. 

The objective in the Brooke Egypt log frame 2010/11 is sustainable improvement in the welfare of working equines in Brooke Egypt’s operational area. 

Reaching sustainable improvement requires long term planning and this review should form the basis for the strategic planning process. 
Also the current log frame refers to potential expansion to El-Sharkia governorate to increase the number of animals reached. 
A strategic review is required to give clear direction to the further development of the programme (including expansion), in line with
organizational strategy, and to ensure the new initiatives are built on current best practices. 

II. Objective of the Review
A strategic review of the Brooke Egypt programmes is planned, in order to look with the BE Board, CEO and senior staff at what BE achieves and delivers 
annually and assess how this contributes to achieving the Brooke’s global programme strategy (see below). 
The outcome of the review should enable the Brooke Egypt management team to conduct strategic planning for the Brooke Egypt and, where needed, adapt its 
programme to ensure that it is in line with the Brooke’s international strategic framework.

The strategic objectives of Brooke most relevant to the Brooke Egypt programme are: 
•	Strategic Objective 1: We will ensure that communities and service providers are enabled to improve and sustain optimal welfare for working 
        equine animals both in the short and long term.
•	Strategic Objective 2: We will significantly increase our impact on working equine animal welfare, within and beyond the geographical areas in 
        which we work, through influencing and collaborating with local, national and international institutions.
•	Strategic Objective 4: We will define and promote our brand with pride and passion, making it synonymous with evidence-based improvements in 
        equine welfare, thereby reinforcing our status as a leading authority on working equine issues.
•	Strategic Objective 5: We will develop an international organization with strong accountability, working effectively and making the best use of 
        knowledge, expertise and resources.

These Terms of Reference are for a programmatic review; an organizational review was conducted in 2009.

III. Scope of the Review
The Review will focus around four (4) programmatic themes, see figure below. 

These themes are derived from the Brooke’s strategic objectives and based on the programmatic approaches required to deliver programmes for 
sustainable improvement in welfare (as described in the Brooke’s international Policy Paper: Making a Sustainable Difference to Working Equine Welfare). 

Figure 1 Strategic review themes.
 

The initial step in reviewing each theme is to identify what is currently done, how far this corresponds with what has been planned and what is achieved 
and delivered annually. 

The main part of the review will consist of an assessment of the effectiveness and efficiency of the approaches in Brooke Egypt to deliver a sustainable 
improvement in the welfare in line with the Brooke’s international strategic objectives and policies. 

The review should identify the gaps, explore options for more effective and efficient ways of working and make recommendations for each theme to be used 
as a basis for strategic planning. 


The Review should address the following questions:
THEME 1 SUSTAINABLE SERVICE PROVISION
•	What animal welfare service provision interventions are currently implemented in the field by the Brooke and how far do these correspond with what has been planned?
•	How effective and efficient is/are the current programme delivery approach(es) to provide veterinary and related services, such as farriery, by Brooke Egypt?  
        Are they working optimally? If not, what options can be explored to improve?
•	Are the range of veterinary, health related and other service providers clearly identified? How effective and efficient are they in delivering Accessibility, 
        Availability, Affordability, Acceptability and Quality (4 As and Q) services to working animals and their owners, users and carers?
•	How effective and efficient is/are the programm(es) in providing sustainable animal welfare services through capacity building of other service providers (4As and Q)?

THEME 2 ENABLING COMMUNITIES TO IMPROVE AND MAINTAIN GOOD ANIMAL WELFARE
•	What situational analysis has been done and what understanding do BE staff have of the animal owners and the animal owning communities?
•	What community engagement interventions are currently implemented in the field by Brooke Egypt and how far do these correspond with what has been planned? 
•	How effective and efficient are the current approaches for programme delivery in changing human behaviour to achieve motivated and knowledgeable owners, users and 
        carers who can improve and sustain good welfare in their working animals?
•	What systems are being built or supported by the programme to ensure sustainability of welfare improvement by the community in the long term; for example creation of 
        groups of animal owners to work together, and establishment of owner-led welfare monitoring systems?
•	Are the communities effectively linked with the service providers in Theme 1, both in the short and long term?

THEME 3 EXPANSION, INFLUENCING AND COLLABORATION 
•	How far has the past expansion targeted the neediest animals of the poorest communities (assessment of the decision-making process around expansion)? 
•	How far has expansion in the past contributed to a strategic development of the programme? What is the added value gained in replication of the regular work, albeit on 
        a larger scale? 
•	Are the collaborations/partnerships leading to, feeding into and/or adding value to the programme approaches to achieve sustainable improvement in animal welfare 
        (enabling communities and/or sustainable service delivery) and if so, how is this done?
•	How far is the wider enabling environment being influenced effectively and efficiently to achieve sustainable improvement of working equine welfare?

THEME 4 MANAGEMENT SYSTEMS AND STRUCTURES 
•	What is Brooke Egypt’s understanding of the external environment and its impact on working equines? How has this understanding been used to inform programming and 
        resource allocation decisions in order to ensure that the programme being implemented is the right one and will contribute to achieving the greatest impact?
•	What are the current systems for project and programme planning, management and implementation? How well are they used and how effective are they in managing 
        implementation and making/informing management and resource allocation decisions?
•	How well are the current staff resources deployed managed and used to develop and implement programmes for working equine welfare improvement?
•	What M&E systems/frameworks exist? How are these used and how effective are they in enabling the monitoring implementation and gauging impact? 
•	What is the role of research in the Brooke Egypt programme? How does it contribute to achieving the organizational objectives? 
        How have the findings of research - past and current – been used to inform programme work?
•	What structures and systems are in place to adhere to the organizational minimum standards, on welfare, partnership and research?

IV. REVIEW PROCESS & METHODOLOGY
Both process and methodology will be discussed and agreed with the consultant at the beginning of the assignment. 
The final ToR will reflect the process and methodology agreed to this point as a result of comments and inputs by a range of stakeholders including the Brooke UK International Department, 
The BE CEO and staff, the BE Board and others.

The review is intended to be participatory, drawing on the knowledge, skills, experience and expertise of BE staff.
The broad process suggested for the review is:
4.1	Preparatory briefing and orientation to the Brooke UK and Brooke Egypt on the strategic review. 
4.2	Studying (desk review) of relevant documents and guidelines (such as the BE annual plan, Brooke strategic framework, monitoring and field visit reports, external clinical 
        audit reports and Brooke policy papers). 
4.3	Developing the review methodology based on the review themes and questions and propose a preliminary work plan with detailed timeline. 
        This detailed work plan should indicate which stakeholder will be consulted, what inputs will be required from them and when.
4.4	Sharing the methodology proposed and preliminary work plan with Brooke Egypt and Brooke UK staff. 
        The methodology and work plan need to be agreed and approved by the Brooke UK Head of Region (BUK HoR) and Brooke Egypt CEO (BE CEO) before proceeding to data collection. 
4.5	Developing required tools /checklist for workshops, focus group discussions (FGDs) and interviews and collecting data based on the work plan, with 
        logistical support from the BE senior team.
4.6	Compiling and analyzing the data collected, assessing both the external and the internal environment (SWOT). 
4.7	Presenting and sharing the preliminary findings and conclusions with BE and BUK staff and Brooke Egypt Board. 
4.8	Preparing and presenting a final draft report with recommendations to the BUK HoR and BE CEO and Brooke Egypt Board.

The Methodology will as a minimum include:
•	Desk review: Assessment of key documents (provided to the consultant).
•	Assessment of ongoing and recently completed animal welfare interventions through visiting the field and meeting stakeholders.
•	In-depth and Semi-structured interviews (SSI) with a representative sample of stakeholders. 
•	Focus Group discussions (FGD) through workshops held with different key stakeholder groups.
The consultant is encouraged to collect both quantitative and qualitative data. 
The key stakeholders are: 
•	Animal-owning communities (primary stakeholders)
•	BE CEO and Board and BE’s staff at all levels, e.g. animal care/ field workers, administrators, vets and senior management staff
•	Local service providers such as farriers, community based health providers and private and government vets
•	BUK HoR and other members of the Brooke International Department in the UK 
Other relevant stakeholders to be consulted will include:
•	Local partners, such as the Spirit of Youth programme (CIDA funded project) 
•	Local, national and international organizations working with and/or interested in equine welfare issues 
•	Government Veterinary and Livestock Departments
•	Other Government departments, Local Administration, Research institutes and Universities related to animal welfare
•	Animal Welfare consortiums and networks such as the Middle Eastern Network for Animal Welfare (MENAW)

The consultant will be required to visit selected regions and clinics, as a minimum the following: Cairo clinic (including a visit to Helwan brick kilns), 
one clinic and field visit in Upper Egypt (Aswan, Luxor and/or Edfu) and a visit to both the Delta operation and the Mersa Matrouh clinic.

V. DELIVERABLES 
5.1 Inception report including the proposed methodology and detailed work plan and timeline. Within two weeks of commencing the review, the consultant(s) 
shall provide and share an inception report ,detailing the proposed methodology and preliminary time-bound work plan. 
This report need to be agreed with the Brooke UK Head of Region and Brooke Egypt CEO before proceeding with the review.
5.2 Mid-term progress report. Within six weeks of the inception report the consultant(s) shall submit a one page mid–term progress report to the BUK HoR and 
Brooke Egypt CEO stating the progress against the work plan as stipulated by the consultant(s) in the inception report. 
5.3 Presentation of preliminary conclusions. Within six weeks of the mid-term progress report the consultant(s) shall present and share the preliminary 
findings and conclusions to a range of stakeholders including BE and BUK staff, BE CEO, BUK HoR and Brooke Egypt Board. 
5.4 Final Draft Report and Presentation. 
Within two weeks of the presentation of the preliminary conclusions, the consultant(s) shall submit a final draft report. 
This report will cover all aspects of the terms of reference and shall be presented to BUK HoR and BE CEO. 
This presentation will offer an opportunity for Brooke Egypt and Brooke UK to make comments which will be incorporated into the final report. 
5.6 Final Report. Within one week of receiving comments on the Final Draft Report, the consultant(s) will produce a final report.
All reports need to be written in English and the Final report must include summaries both in English and in Arabic.
The consultants shall be directly supervised and supported by the BUK HoR or a person designated by the HoR. 
The HoR will keep the BE CEO informed of progress through regular phone briefings held once a week followed by an e-mail. 
Brooke Egypt will appoint a senior staff member to provide logistical support to the selected consultant(s), such as arrangement of accommodation and local travel in Egypt. 

VI TIMING
The estimated time for the study, including submission of final report, will 40 to 45 days over a period of four (4) months. 
The review should commence by 1st of June 2010 so that the findings can be delivered by the end of September. 
This will give both Brooke Egypt and the Brooke UK time to consider the findings and use them to undertake and complete strategic planning in time for the annual planning in November.




VII. SELECTION CRITERIA 
The selection criteria for the consultant(s)/agency are:
Essential

•	Proven track record of undertaking and facilitating strategic programmatic review processes for Non-Government Organizations
•	Understanding of and experience in all phases of NGO project cycle management and implementation
•	Experience in conducting interviews and in organizing and conducting field surveys including Focus Group Discussions 
•	Capacity to identify, collect and analyze quantitative and qualitative data to produce clear, well argued, validated and concise recommendations linked to 
        evidence/findings of the review.
•	Excellent interpersonal skills and an ability to work with Brooke Egypt field staff, local communities and government representatives
•	Strong presentation and writing skills in English 



Desirable 
•	Previous experience of working in Egypt
•	Knowledge of the Brooke’s Programmatic approaches
•	A commitment to animal welfare
•	Understanding of veterinary /livestock programmes, and the livestock schemes and policies of Government and Non-Government Organisations working in Egypt
•	Working knowledge of Arabic is highly desirable

It is assumed that the consultant(s) will be able to rapidly establish their non-biased/neutrality to range of stakeholders. 
VII. Expression of Interest 
Qualified and interested consultants/agencies should submit their expression of interest no later than 6th May 2010.
This should include  
(i)	CVs of the Consultant(s) expected to undertake the work; 
(ii)    A list of related consultancies/ contracts 
        carried out satisfactorily, supported by credentials, plus two referees from organisations for whom the consultant(s)/agency has carried out         work very recently;
(iii)	Confirmation of the capacity to deliver the completed work in the set timeframe; 
(iv)	A brief description of the suggested process and methodology for the review, based on this ToR (maximum 1 page);
(v)	A financial proposal stating the estimated numbers of days required for the review and fee proposed.
